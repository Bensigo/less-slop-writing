# AI Slop Taxonomy

Use this reference for detailed audits and rewrites. The taxonomy is adapted from Momentic's 34 AI-slop patterns and organized by the kind of damage each pattern causes.

Source: https://momenticmarketing.com/blog/avoid-ai-slop

## Word-Level Slop

1. **Significance amplifiers**: Words that make ordinary changes sound historic. Flag "pivotal", "game-changing", "transformative", "revolutionary", "groundbreaking" when the facts do not justify them.
2. **Tourism and heritage language**: Travel-brochure framing for normal businesses, teams, cities, or offices. Flag "nestled", "vibrant", "rich heritage", "hidden gem", "bustling".
3. **Hedge words**: Claim-weakening words that protect the sentence from itself. Flag "arguably", "fairly", "somewhat", "notable", "worth noting", "tends to".
4. **Filler qualifiers**: Intensifiers that add no information. Flag "really", "truly", "incredibly", "very", "deeply" unless they change the claim.
5. **Sycophantic openers**: Fake praise or service language before answering. Flag "great question", "happy to help", "I'd be happy to", "excellent point".
6. **AI transition words**: Over-formal connectors. Flag "moreover", "furthermore", "additionally", "in addition", "nevertheless" when simpler transitions work.
7. **Verb inflation**: Fancy verbs replacing normal verbs. Flag "leverage", "utilize", "craft", "delve", "bolster", "foster", "facilitate", "empower", "unlock".

## Phrase-Level Slop

8. **Meta-commentary**: Announcing the point instead of making it. Flag "it is important to note", "the key takeaway is", "this article will explore".
9. **Real-truth constructions**: Fake wisdom windups. Flag "at the end of the day", "what it really comes down to", "the reality is", "the truth is".
10. **Manufactured hooks**: Generic broad openings. Flag "in today's rapidly evolving landscape", "now more than ever", "in an increasingly digital world".
11. **Mid-narrative pivots**: Manufactured drama. Flag "here's where it gets interesting", "but that's not all", "the twist".
12. **Negative parallelism**: "Not X, but Y" structures that replace a concrete point with a vague grander one.
13. **Collaborative language**: Fake shared action between writer and reader. Flag "let's dive in", "let's explore", "let's unpack", "join us as".
14. **Corporate jargon**: Business phrases that gesture at value without naming work. Flag "content ecosystem", "move the needle", "drive outcomes", "unlock scalable growth", "align strategy".

## Structural Slop

15. **Reflexive rule of three**: Forced three-item lists when the evidence does not naturally produce three items.
16. **Staccato clusters**: Several short sentences used for synthetic drama rather than clarity.
17. **Section-ending summaries**: Repeating what the section already proved.
18. **Rigid outline structure**: School-essay sequencing: "first", "second", "third", "in conclusion" when the piece needs a stronger organizing idea.
19. **Default redemption arc**: Turning every failure into a lesson, opportunity, or growth story.
20. **Question restating**: Repeating the prompt before answering it.
21. **Excessive bulleting**: Using bullets to make thin claims look useful.
22. **Conclusion restating intro**: Ending by rephrasing the opening instead of adding consequence, decision, or next action.

## Tone Slop

23. **Relentless positivity**: Everything is exciting, incredible, powerful, or an opportunity.
24. **Excessive formality**: Stiff phrases that create distance. Flag "please do not hesitate", "should you require", "at your earliest convenience".
25. **Manufactured vulnerability**: Performed honesty. Flag "I'll be honest", "truth be told", "to be transparent" when it is not needed.
26. **Uniform enthusiasm**: Same emotional weight for minor updates and major announcements.
27. **Apologetic hedging**: Too many caveats before a useful observation.

## Formatting Slop

28. **Em dash overuse**: Too many interruptions in one paragraph or sentence.
29. **Bold as decoration**: Random bold phrases inside body copy that do not function as headings.
30. **Emoji in professional contexts**: Emoji used to create fake energy in serious business writing.

## Content Slop

31. **Vague attribution**: "Research suggests", "studies show", "experts say" without naming a source.
32. **Semantic hollowness**: Smart-sounding sentences with no action, claim, example, or evidence.
33. **Explaining significance**: "This matters because" used as a label instead of showing the consequence.
34. **Avoiding specifics**: Missing names, numbers, dates, examples, constraints, before/after states, or actual mechanisms.

## Practical Tests

- If the sentence were deleted, would the reader lose concrete information?
- Does the sentence name who, what, when, where, how many, or why?
- Could a skeptical reader verify the claim?
- Is the emotional intensity proportional to the event?
- Is a fancy word hiding a simple action?
- Does the structure help the reader, or does it merely sound organized?
